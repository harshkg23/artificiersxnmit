Lung Cancer Overview

Detection Methods

Lung cancer detection typically begins with a thorough medical history and physical examination by a healthcare professional. If lung cancer is suspected based on symptoms or risk factors such as smoking history, the next step usually involves imaging tests such as chest X-rays, CT scans, or PET scans. These tests help visualize the lungs and any abnormalities that may indicate the presence of cancerous growths or tumors. In some cases, a biopsy may be performed to collect tissue samples from the lungs for further analysis under a microscope. Additionally, advanced imaging techniques such as MRI or bronchoscopy may be utilized to provide detailed views of the lungs and surrounding tissues. Early detection of lung cancer is crucial for effective treatment, and prompt diagnosis allows for timely intervention and improved outcomes for patients."

Common Symptoms

The symptoms of lung cancer often manifest as subtle signs that may be overlooked or attributed to other causes, especially in the early stages of the disease. Persistent coughing that worsens over time, coughing up blood or rust-colored sputum, and chronic chest pain or discomfort are among the most common symptoms. Shortness of breath, wheezing, and hoarseness may also occur, particularly as the cancer progresses and affects lung function. Some individuals may experience unexplained weight loss, loss of appetite, fatigue, or recurrent respiratory infections. Additionally, lung cancer can cause bone pain, headaches, and swelling in the neck or face if the cancer spreads to other parts of the body. It's essential to recognize these symptoms and seek medical attention promptly for further evaluation and diagnosis

Types of Lung Cancer

Lung cancer comprises two main types: non-small cell lung cancer (NSCLC) and small cell lung cancer (SCLC). NSCLC includes subtypes like adenocarcinoma, squamous cell carcinoma, and large cell carcinoma, with adenocarcinoma being the most common. SCLC, accounting for about 15% of cases, is aggressive and characterized by small, rapidly spreading cancer cells, strongly linked to smoking. These distinctions guide treatment and prognosis.

Treatment Options

Treatment options for lung cancer are diverse and tailored to individual cases. Surgery involves removing the tumor and nearby tissue, while chemotherapy uses drugs to kill cancer cells throughout the body. Radiation therapy targets cancer cells with high-energy rays, often used in combination with surgery or chemotherapy. Targeted therapy focuses on specific abnormalities in cancer cells, while immunotherapy boosts the body's immune system to fight cancer. These treatments may be used alone or in combination, depending on factors such as cancer type, stage, and patient health.
